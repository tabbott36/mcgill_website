Abbott Radio Telescope

The Abbott Radio Telescope (ART) is currently under construction! Please check out the Blog for updates! Motivation The Abbott Radio Telescope began as an ambitious project that I hoped to use mostly as a learning experience and a test of my engineering abilities. Soon after, I realized it had great potential as a bridge between amateur and professional radio astronomy. The motivation for the Abbott Radio Telescope is twofold:  To offer students, amateurs, and early-career astronomers the ability to have time on a proper radio telescope where they can learn how to use it, collect data, and perform meaningful analysis.  To offer the general public (particularly amateur radio astronomers) opportunities to learn about radio astronomy and to have open access to the design, software, and data collected at ART. Summary ART will be a 20m dish on an alt-az mount, making it the largest fully-steerable amateur telescope in the world. Initially, the telescope will be fitted with a 1420MHz receiver to observe the spin-flip transition of hydrogen. Depending on my financial situation by the end of this project (and structural constraints), the ART will be fitted with upgraded receivers that cover a much wider range of the radio spectrum. Brief History Below is the original build site - my backyard! Surrounded by mountains and in a sparsely populated region of Southern Quebec makes this a great location for a radio telescope. The 85 thousand pound concrete foundation provides an incredible amount of stability to allow this dish to point very accurately. In addition, a 7x7 meter steel base makes for a stable platform to build the tower off of. To bring the dish high off the ground and provide it with enough space to tilt, a 6 meter tower was built above the platform. With no source of electricity, a clever and efficient use of manual labor is crucial to completing the project. After raising the walls, floor joists an bracing was added inside the tower. The tower will also house the electronics used to filter and amplify the data. Once observations begin, all software and data will be publicly available. A temporary roof built over the tower as the mount (not shown) is currently being constructed.  Acknowledgments The name "Abbott Radio Telescope" is in honor of my family whose creativity and expertise have been invaluable throughout the construction of this telescope.
